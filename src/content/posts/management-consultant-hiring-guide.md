Hiring a management consultant is one of the highest-leverage moves available to a growing business — and one of the most commonly mismanaged. The failure mode isn't usually the consultant. It's the brief.

## Start With the Problem, Not the Solution

The most common mistake: hiring a management consultant and saying "here's our org chart, make it better." That's like going to a doctor and asking them to prescribe medication before they've diagnosed you.

A useful brief answers:
- What has changed in the business or market that prompted this engagement?
- What decision are you trying to make, or what outcome are you trying to reach?
- What have you already tried, and why didn't it work?
- What does success look like in 90 days?

## Selecting the Right Consultant

Management consulting is broad. Be wary of generalists who claim equal expertise in finance, operations, marketing, and HR. The best consultants are strong in 1–2 areas and know when to bring in specialists.

### Questions that separate good consultants from mediocre ones:

- "Walk me through your diagnostic process for an engagement like this."
- "What's the most difficult client relationship you've managed, and how did it end?"
- "What would make this engagement fail?"

The third question is the most revealing. A confident, experienced consultant will give you a specific, honest answer. A nervous one will dodge it.

## Structuring the Engagement

Management consulting engagements should have three phases:

**Phase 1 — Discovery (1–3 weeks):** Stakeholder interviews, data review, and hypothesis formation. Output: a clear problem statement and a proposed approach.

**Phase 2 — Analysis and recommendation (2–6 weeks):** Deep-dive into the highest-priority areas. Output: recommendations with evidence and implementation roadmap.

**Phase 3 — Implementation support (optional, 4–12 weeks):** Supporting the internal team to execute. Output: specific deliverables defined at the start of this phase.

Don't let Phase 3 turn into an indefinite retainer. Define it upfront.

## Managing the Relationship

Assign an internal owner to every consulting engagement — someone with authority to make decisions and access to data. Without an empowered internal counterpart, consultants waste cycles navigating internal politics instead of delivering value.

Weekly check-ins (30 minutes maximum) keep engagement on track without consuming the consultant's billable time on internal meetings.

## When to Walk Away

Exit a consulting engagement early if:
- The consultant is consistently repackaging your own input and presenting it back as insight
- Deliverables are consistently late without explanation
- The team is growing disengaged from the process

A signed contract is not a reason to continue a failing engagement. Cut losses early — the reputational damage of a failed engagement outlasts the financial hit.
