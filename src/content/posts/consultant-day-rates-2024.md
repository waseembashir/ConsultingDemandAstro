Consulting fees are notoriously opaque. Consultants rarely publish rates, clients rarely share what they paid, and the range — even within the same specialism — can span 4x from junior to senior.

This guide publishes the actual market data, sourced from ConsultingDemand's 2024 survey of 340 UK-based consultants and 180 client businesses.

## UK Day Rates by Specialism (2024)

| Specialism | Junior | Mid-Level | Senior | Principal/Partner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Management Consulting | £500–£800 | £900–£1,400 | £1,400–£2,200 | £2,200–£4,000+ |
| Strategy | £600–£900 | £1,000–£1,600 | £1,600–£2,500 | £2,500–£5,000 |
| Marketing Consulting | £400–£700 | £700–£1,200 | £1,200–£1,900 | £1,800–£3,500 |

All figures in GBP. Rates are excluding VAT.

## What Drives Rate Variation

Within each band, four factors move the needle most significantly:

**Sector depth.** A financial services consultant charges more than a generalist because their knowledge is harder to replicate. Expect 15–30% premium for deep sector specialists.

**Geographic market.** London-based consultants charge 20–35% more than equivalent practitioners in other UK cities. This gap is narrowing as remote work normalises, but persists for on-site-heavy engagements.

**Deliverable clarity.** Consultants who quote project fees rather than day rates often earn more per-day equivalent — because clear deliverables reduce scope creep risk for both parties.

**Firm affiliation.** Independent consultants typically charge 30–50% less than equivalent talent at branded boutiques, and 60–80% less than tier-one firms. The gap reflects brand premium and overhead recovery, not necessarily quality.

## Negotiation Reality

Consultants set rates based on what the market will bear — not purely on cost plus margin. Rate negotiation is entirely normal and expected.

Effective negotiation tactics:
- Offering a longer initial commitment (3 months vs 1 month) in exchange for a rate discount
- Requesting a fixed-fee project rather than open day rate (gives you cost certainty, gives them upside on efficiency)
- Bundling multiple smaller projects into one larger engagement

What doesn't work: trying to dramatically undercut market rate. The best consultants have enough demand to decline under-market engagements. You'll end up with whoever's available.
